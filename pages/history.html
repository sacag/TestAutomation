Microsoft History

Microsoft's journey from a two-person startup to one of the world's most valuable companies spans five decades of relentless innovation, strategic acquisitions, and cultural transformation. This timeline captures the pivotal moments that shaped the company.

1975 - The Beginning

Bill Gates and Paul Allen founded Microsoft on April 4, 1975 in Albuquerque, New Mexico. Their initial product was a BASIC interpreter for the Altair 8800 microcomputer. The name "Microsoft" was coined by Allen as a portmanteau of "microcomputer" and "software."

1980 - The IBM Deal

Microsoft struck its most consequential early deal with IBM to provide an operating system for IBM's first personal computer. Microsoft acquired QDOS (Quick and Dirty Operating System), renamed it MS-DOS, and licensed it to IBM. Crucially, Microsoft retained the right to license MS-DOS to other manufacturers, a decision that generated enormous revenue as the PC market exploded.

1985 - Windows 1.0 Launch

Microsoft launched Windows 1.0, a graphical extension of MS-DOS that introduced mouse-driven navigation to mainstream PC users. Though initially limited, Windows established the foundation for the graphical user interface that would dominate personal computing for decades.

1986 - IPO and Wealth Creation

Microsoft went public on March 13, 1986, at $21 per share. The IPO created three billionaires and an estimated 12,000 millionaires among Microsoft employees. Bill Gates, at 30 years old, became one of the youngest self-made billionaires in history.

1990 - Microsoft Office

Microsoft introduced the Office suite for Windows, bundling Word, Excel, and PowerPoint into a single package. This move fundamentally changed the productivity software market and created a recurring revenue business that remains central to Microsoft's success today.

1995 - Windows 95 and the Internet Era

Windows 95 launched to massive fanfare, selling seven million copies in its first five weeks. The release also marked Microsoft's pivot toward the internet, bundling Internet Explorer and positioning Windows as a networked operating system.

2001 - Xbox and Windows XP

Microsoft entered the gaming console market with the Xbox, launching a new competitive front against Sony PlayStation and Nintendo. The same year, Windows XP unified Microsoft's consumer and business operating system lines and became one of the most widely used operating systems in history, with many organizations running it well into the 2010s.

2008 - Failed Yahoo Bid

Microsoft made a $44.6 billion bid to acquire Yahoo in an attempt to compete with Google in search and online advertising. Yahoo rejected the offer, and Microsoft eventually developed its own search engine, Bing, launched in 2009.

2011 - Skype Acquisition

Microsoft acquired Skype for $8.5 billion, the largest acquisition in the company's history at that time. Skype's communications technology was later integrated into Microsoft Teams and other Microsoft products.

2014 - The Nadella Era Begins

Satya Nadella became Microsoft's third CEO, succeeding Steve Ballmer. Nadella immediately began repositioning Microsoft around cloud computing with Microsoft Azure as the central pillar. His "mobile-first, cloud-first" strategy reversed years of stagnation and reignited Microsoft's stock performance and cultural reputation.

2016 - LinkedIn Acquisition

Microsoft acquired LinkedIn for $26.2 billion, adding the world's largest professional social network to its portfolio. LinkedIn now integrates deeply with Microsoft 365, Dynamics CRM, and Teams, creating unique enterprise networking capabilities.

2018 - GitHub Acquisition

Microsoft acquired GitHub for $7.5 billion, making it the owner of the world's largest software development platform with over 28 million developers. The acquisition, which many initially viewed skeptically, has since been celebrated as Microsoft committed to keeping GitHub independent and open-source-friendly.

2020 - Teams and Remote Work

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated adoption of Microsoft Teams, which grew from 20 million daily active users in November 2019 to 115 million by October 2020. Teams became a central hub of the remote work revolution and a critical component of Microsoft 365.

2023 - AI Leadership with OpenAI

Microsoft deepened its partnership with OpenAI, announcing a multi-billion dollar investment and integrating OpenAI's GPT models across its product portfolio. Microsoft Copilot was launched across Office, Azure, GitHub, Bing, and Windows, positioning Microsoft as the leading enterprise AI company.

2024 - Activision Blizzard Acquisition

Microsoft completed its acquisition of Activision Blizzard for approximately $68.7 billion, the largest acquisition in gaming history. The deal brought franchises such as Call of Duty, World of Warcraft, Candy Crush, and Overwatch into Microsoft's portfolio, making Xbox the world's largest gaming company by revenue. The acquisition faced extensive regulatory scrutiny in multiple jurisdictions before finally closing in October 2023.

Today

Microsoft regularly ranks among the two or three most valuable companies in the world by market capitalization. The company's cloud and AI businesses continue to grow rapidly, while its traditional software businesses provide a stable, highly profitable foundation.
